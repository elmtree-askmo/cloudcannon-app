question: >-
    What is the genetic basis for the AB blood type expression?
answer: >-
    The genetic basis for the expression of the AB blood type is primarily determined by the presence of multiple alleles and the phenomenon of codominance. In the ABO blood group system, there are three alleles: \n\n- **IA** (which codes for A antigens)\n- **IB** (which codes for B antigens)\n- **i** (which does not code for any antigens)\n\nIndividuals inherit one allele from each parent, leading to the following possible combinations and corresponding blood types:\n\n- **IAIA or IAi** results in blood type A (presence of A antigens)\n- **IBIB or IBi** results in blood type B (presence of B antigens)\n- **IAIB** results in blood type AB (presence of both A and B antigens)\n- **ii** results in blood type O (absence of A and B antigens)\n\nIn the case of blood type AB, both the IA and IB alleles are expressed simultaneously. This is an example of codominance, where both alleles contribute equally to the phenotype, resulting in the presence of both A and B antigens on the surface of red blood cells. \n\nThe presence of these antigens is crucial for determining blood type compatibility, especially in medical situations such as blood transfusions, where mismatched blood types can trigger an immune response. \n\nIn summary, the AB blood type expression is a result of the inheritance of both IA and IB alleles, showcasing the principles of multiple alleles and codominance in genetics.

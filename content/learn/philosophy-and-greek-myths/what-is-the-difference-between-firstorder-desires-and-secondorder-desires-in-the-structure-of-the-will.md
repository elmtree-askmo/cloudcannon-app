question: >-
    What is the difference between first-order desires and second-order desires in the structure of the will?
answer: >-
    The distinction between first-order desires and second-order desires is crucial in understanding the structure of the will, particularly in the context of free will and moral responsibility.\n\n1. **First-order desires** are the basic, immediate desires that motivate an individual to perform certain actions. They are straightforward and relate directly to what one wants to do. For example, a person may have a first-order desire to eat a piece of cake or to go for a run. These desires are about specific actions or outcomes.\n\n2. **Second-order desires**, on the other hand, are desires about one's first-order desires. They reflect an individual's preferences regarding their own motivations. For instance, a person may have a first-order desire to smoke but also a second-order desire to not want to smoke. This indicates a conflict between their immediate desires (to smoke) and their long-term goals or values (to be healthy). \n\nThe relationship between these two types of desires is significant for the concept of free will. A coherent structure of the will involves harmony between first-order and second-order desires. For example, if someone desires to exercise (first-order) and also desires to want to exercise (second-order), their will is structured harmoniously, allowing them to act freely in accordance with their values.\n\nIn summary, first-order desires are about direct actions, while second-order desires are about the desires themselves, reflecting a deeper level of self-reflection and evaluation. This distinction is essential for understanding how individuals navigate conflicts between their immediate impulses and their broader goals or values.

question: >-
    What does it mean to scale a vector, and how is it done?
answer: >-
    Scaling a vector refers to the process of changing its length while maintaining its direction. This is akin to resizing an image: the overall shape remains the same, but the dimensions can be increased or decreased. When a vector is scaled by a scalar (a numerical value), each component of the vector is multiplied by that scalar.\n\nFor example, if we have a vector represented as a column matrix:\n\n$$\n\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\n$$\n\nand we scale it by a scalar factor $k$, the new vector $\mathbf{v'}$ becomes:\n\n$$\n\mathbf{v'} = k \cdot \mathbf{v} = k \cdot \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} kx \\ ky \end{pmatrix}\n$$\n\nThis operation alters the magnitude of the vector while keeping its direction unchanged. For instance, if $k = 0.5$, the new vector will have half the magnitude of the original vector, while if $k = 2$, the new vector will have double the magnitude.\n\nIn practical applications, scaling vectors is useful in various fields such as physics and engineering, where it can represent changes in quantities like force or velocity without altering their direction. Understanding how to scale vectors is essential for solving problems involving vector addition, resolution, and other vector operations.

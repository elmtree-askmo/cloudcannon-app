question: >-
    How do polynomial and rational functions differ in terms of their behaviour?
answer: >-
    Polynomial and rational functions exhibit distinct behaviors due to their definitions and structural characteristics. Hereâ€™s a detailed comparison of their differences:\n\n### 1. Definition\n- **Polynomial Functions**: A polynomial function is expressed in the form \n  $$ \n  P(x) = a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \ldots + a_1 x + a_0 \n  $$\n  where $ a_n, a_{n-1}, \ldots, a_0 $ are constants, $ n $ is a non-negative integer, and $ x $ is a variable. The degree of the polynomial is determined by the highest power of $ x $.\n\n- **Rational Functions**: A rational function is defined as the ratio of two polynomial functions, expressed as \n  $$ \n  R(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \n  $$\n  where \( P(x) \) and \( Q(x) \) are polynomials and \( Q(x) \neq 0 \).\n\n### 2. Domain\n- **Polynomial Functions**: The domain of polynomial functions is all real numbers, $ \mathbb{R} $, since they are defined for every value of $ x $.\n\n- **Rational Functions**: The domain of rational functions is restricted. They are undefined at values of $ x $ that make the denominator \( Q(x) = 0 \), leading to vertical asymptotes or holes in the graph.\n\n### 3. End Behavior\n- **Polynomial Functions**: The end behavior of polynomial functions is influenced by the degree and the leading coefficient. For example:\n  - If the degree is even and the leading coefficient is positive, the ends of the graph will rise to positive infinity as $ x $ approaches both positive and negative infinity.\n  - If the degree is odd and the leading coefficient is positive, the graph will rise to positive infinity as $ x $ approaches positive infinity and fall to negative infinity as $ x $ approaches negative infinity.\n\n- **Rational Functions**: The end behavior of rational functions can vary significantly. They may approach horizontal asymptotes, which are determined by the degrees of the numerator and denominator:\n  - If the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator, the horizontal asymptote is $ y = 0 $.\n  - If the degrees are equal, the horizontal asymptote is $ y = \frac{a}{b} $, where $ a $ and $ b $ are the leading coefficients of the numerator and denominator, respectively.\n  - If the degree of the numerator is greater than the degree of the denominator, there is no horizontal asymptote (the function may approach infinity).\n\n### 4. Graph Characteristics\n- **Polynomial Functions**: The graphs of polynomial functions are continuous and smooth, with no breaks, holes, or asymptotes. They can have multiple turning points, which correspond to local maxima and minima.\n\n- **Rational Functions**: The graphs of rational functions can have vertical asymptotes (where the function approaches infinity) and horizontal or slant asymptotes. They may also have holes in the graph where the function is undefined.\n\n### 5. Examples\n- **Polynomial Function**: An example is \( f(x) = 2x^3 - 4x + 1 \), which is defined for all $ x $ and has a smooth graph.\n  \n- **Rational Function**: An example is \( g(x) = \frac{x^2 + 1}{x - 3} \), which is undefined at $ x = 3 $ and has a vertical asymptote there.\n\n### Conclusion\nIn summary, polynomial functions are defined for all real numbers and exhibit smooth, continuous behavior, while rational functions have restrictions in their domains and can exhibit asymptotic behavior. Understanding these differences is crucial for analyzing their graphs and behaviors in calculus and algebra.

question: >-
    What is a median in a triangle, and what are its properties?
answer: >-
    A median in a triangle is a line segment that connects a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. Each triangle has three medians, and they possess several important properties:\n\n1. **Intersection at the Centroid**: The three medians of a triangle intersect at a single point called the centroid. This point is the center of mass of the triangle and has the property that it divides each median into two segments, with one segment being twice the length of the other. Specifically, if a median is divided by the centroid, the segment connecting the vertex to the centroid is twice as long as the segment connecting the centroid to the midpoint of the opposite side.\n\n2. **Divides Triangle into Equal Areas**: Each median divides the triangle into two smaller triangles of equal area. This means that if you draw a median, the area of the triangle on one side of the median is equal to the area of the triangle on the other side.\n\n3. **Number of Medians**: Every triangle has exactly three medians, one from each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side.\n\n4. **Length of Medians**: The lengths of the medians can be calculated using the formula derived from the coordinates of the triangle's vertices. If the vertices of the triangle are given by coordinates \(A(x_1, y_1)\), \(B(x_2, y_2)\), and \(C(x_3, y_3)\), the length of the median from vertex $A$ to the midpoint $M$ of side $BC$ can be calculated as follows:\n\n   $$ \n   M = \left( \frac{x_2 + x_3}{2}, \frac{y_2 + y_3}{2} \right) \n   $$\n\n   The length of the median $AM$ can then be found using the distance formula:\n\n   $$\n   AM = \sqrt{\left(x_1 - \frac{x_2 + x_3}{2}\right)^2 + \left(y_1 - \frac{y_2 + y_3}{2}\right)^2}\n   $$\n\n5. **Centroid Location**: The centroid can also be found using the average of the vertices' coordinates:\n\n   $$\n   G = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2 + x_3}{3}, \frac{y_1 + y_2 + y_3}{3} \right)\n   $$\n\nThese properties make medians a fundamental concept in triangle geometry, useful in various applications such as area calculations and understanding the balance of shapes.

    How do reflections maintain the size and shape of geometric figures?

This results in a mirror image of the original shape.\n\n2. **Line of Reflection**: The line of reflection acts like a mirror. Common lines of reflection include the x-axis, y-axis, and the line $y = x$. The position of the line determines how the figure is flipped.\n\n3. **Isometric Transformation**: Reflection is classified as an isometric transformation. This means that the distances between points in the original figure and the reflected figure are preserved. Consequently, the original figure and its reflection are congruent, meaning they have the same size and shape.\n\n4. **Properties of Reflections**:\n   - **Congruence**: The image (reflected figure) and the pre-image (original figure) are congruent. This means that all corresponding sides and angles are equal.\n   - **Orientation**: While the size and shape are preserved, the orientation of the figure is reversed. For example, if a figure is oriented in a certain direction, its reflection will face the opposite direction.\n   - **Distance Preservation**: The distance between any two points in the original figure is equal to the distance between their corresponding points in the reflected figure.\n\n### Example\n\nConsider a triangle with vertices at points A(1, 2), B(3, 4), and C(5, 2) reflected across the y-axis. The coordinates of the reflected triangle will be A'(-1, 2), B'(-3, 4), and C'(-5, 2). \n\n- **Size and Shape**: The lengths of the sides of triangle ABC will be equal to the lengths of the sides of triangle A'B'C', confirming that the size and shape are maintained.\n- **Orientation**: The orientation of triangle A'B'C' will be a mirror image of triangle ABC.\n\n### Conclusion\n\nIn summary, reflections maintain the size and shape of geometric figures because they are isometric transformations that preserve distances and angles, resulting in congruent figures. The only change is the orientation, which is reversed, creating a mirror image of the original figure.

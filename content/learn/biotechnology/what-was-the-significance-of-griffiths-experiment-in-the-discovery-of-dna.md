question: >-
    What was the significance of Griffith's Experiment in the discovery of DNA?
answer: >-
    Griffith's Experiment, conducted in 1928 by British geneticist Frederick Griffith, was a pivotal moment in the discovery of DNA as the genetic material. The experiment involved two strains of the bacterium *Streptococcus pneumoniae*: a virulent strain (type III-S) and a non-virulent strain (type II-R). Griffith injected mice with these strains and observed that when the heat-killed virulent bacteria were mixed with live non-virulent bacteria, the non-virulent bacteria were transformed into virulent ones, leading to the death of the mice. This phenomenon was termed the "transforming principle."\n\nThe significance of Griffith's Experiment lies in its demonstration that some factor from the dead virulent bacteria could convert the non-virulent bacteria into a virulent form. Although Griffith did not identify this factor, his work laid the groundwork for future research into the biochemical nature of genetic material. It redirected scientific inquiry away from the then-dominant tetranucleotide hypothesis, which incorrectly suggested that proteins were the genetic material.\n\nGriffith's findings prompted further investigation by scientists such as Oswald Avery, Colin MacLeod, and Maclyn McCarty, who, in 1944, confirmed that DNA was indeed the transforming principle responsible for heredity. Thus, Griffith's Experiment was crucial in establishing the foundation for molecular genetics and the understanding that DNA is the carrier of genetic information, fundamentally changing the course of biology and medicine.

question: >-
    What distinguishes Category A agents from Category B and C agents?
answer: >-
    The classification of bioterrorism agents into Categories A, B, and C by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) is based on their ease of dissemination and the severity of the illnesses they cause. Hereâ€™s a detailed distinction among these categories:\n\n### Category A Agents\n- **Risk Level**: Highest priority.\n- **Ease of Spread**: Easily disseminated or transmitted from person to person.\n- **Severity of Illness**: High mortality rates and significant potential for public health impact.\n- **Examples**: Agents such as Bacillus anthracis (anthrax), Yersinia pestis (plague), and Variola major (smallpox).\n- **Public Health Impact**: These agents pose a significant threat to national security and require special action for public health preparedness.\n\n### Category B Agents\n- **Risk Level**: Second highest priority.\n- **Ease of Spread**: Moderately easy to disseminate.\n- **Severity of Illness**: Cause moderate rates of disease with low death rates.\n- **Examples**: Agents like Brucella spp. (brucellosis) and Coxiella burnetii (Q fever).\n- **Public Health Impact**: While they can threaten food and water safety, they are less likely to cause widespread panic or social disruption compared to Category A agents.\n\n### Category C Agents\n- **Risk Level**: Third highest priority.\n- **Ease of Spread**: Emerging pathogens that could be engineered for mass dissemination.\n- **Severity of Illness**: Potential for high mortality and morbidity rates, but currently less known or established than Category A and B agents.\n- **Examples**: Includes emerging infectious diseases and pathogens that could be modified for enhanced dissemination, such as Nipah virus or certain antibiotic-resistant bacteria.\n- **Public Health Impact**: These agents are considered future threats and may pose significant risks due to their potential for rapid spread and high impact.\n\nIn summary, the primary distinctions lie in the agents' potential for dissemination and the severity of the diseases they cause, with Category A agents being the most dangerous and requiring the most stringent preparedness measures.

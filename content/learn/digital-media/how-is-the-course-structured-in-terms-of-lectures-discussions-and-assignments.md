question: >-
    How is the course structured in terms of lectures, discussions, and assignments?
answer: >-
    The course on Media Institutions is structured to provide a comprehensive learning experience through a combination of lectures, discussions, and assignments. Hereâ€™s an overview of the course structure:\n\n1. **Lectures and Readings**: The course primarily relies on lectures that introduce key concepts and theories related to media, culture, and communication. Students are expected to engage with assigned readings that complement the lecture material, allowing them to explore critical debates in the field.\n\n2. **Discussions**: Class discussions play a significant role in the course. Students participate in discussions that encourage them to synthesize information from lectures and readings, fostering a deeper understanding of the material. Weekly discussion sections are designed to facilitate engagement with the content and with peers.\n\n3. **Assignments**: The course includes various assignments that contribute to the overall learning objectives:\n   - **Essays**: Students are required to complete four essays throughout the semester, which allow them to connect course content with their own experiences and insights.\n   - **Short Writing Exercises**: These assignments help students practice their writing skills and reinforce the concepts discussed in class.\n   - **Oral Presentations**: Students will also engage in oral presentations, which are critical for developing communication skills in the humanities and social sciences.\n   - **Media Project**: In groups, students will work on a media project that culminates in a presentation at the end of the semester. This project requires collaboration and application of research interests and technical design.\n\n4. **Quizzes and Exams**: Regular quizzes are scheduled to assess students' understanding of the material covered in the weeks leading up to the quiz. A final exam is also part of the course, which must be taken in person or with a proctor.\n\n5. **Grading**: The grading structure typically includes a combination of essay scores, participation in discussions, performance on quizzes, and the final project presentation. Attendance and participation are also factored into the final grade.\n\nOverall, the course is designed to encourage active engagement with the material through a variety of formats, ensuring that students develop both critical thinking and practical communication skills.

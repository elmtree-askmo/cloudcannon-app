question: >-
    What is the historical context of deception detection tests in India?
answer: >-
    The historical context of deception detection tests in India is rich and varied, tracing back to ancient practices and evolving into modern scientific techniques. \n\nIn ancient India, methods for detecting deception were quite rudimentary and often relied on psychological and physiological responses. One notable technique involved the "sacred ass/donkey test," where suspects were told that an animal would bray if a guilty person grabbed its tail. Another method involved stuffing a suspect's mouth with dry rice; if the rice stuck to the mouth, it was interpreted as a sign of guilt. These early practices highlight the belief that physiological signs could indicate a person's psychological state when lying.\n\nThe evolution of deception detection techniques continued over the centuries, leading to more structured approaches. The first significant scientific method akin to modern lie detection was introduced in India in 1936 with the narco-analysis test, which involves administering a drug to induce a state of suggestibility in the subject. However, it wasn't until 2002 that this technique gained notable attention during the Godhra Carnage Case. \n\nIn contemporary India, deception detection tests (DDTs) such as polygraph tests, narco-analysis, and brain mapping have been employed in criminal investigations. Despite their use, the ethical implications and scientific validity of these tests have been subjects of debate. The Supreme Court of India has set strict guidelines regarding their administration, emphasizing that such tests should only be conducted with the consent of the individual and cannot be used as direct evidence in court. A landmark ruling in 2010 reinforced the need to respect individual rights and privacy, yet concerns about the invasive nature and reliability of these tests persist.\n\nOverall, the historical context of deception detection tests in India reflects a transition from primitive methods to more sophisticated scientific techniques, while also grappling with the ethical and legal challenges that accompany their use in modern investigative practices.

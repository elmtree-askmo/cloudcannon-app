question: >-
    What is the definition of classical conditioning in psychology?
answer: >-
    Classical conditioning, also known as Pavlovian or respondent conditioning, is a learning process in which a biologically potent stimulus is paired with a previously neutral stimulus to elicit a conditioned response. This form of learning was first studied and conceptualized by Russian physiologist Ivan Pavlov in the late 19th century. \n\nIn classical conditioning, the unconditioned stimulus (US), which naturally elicits a response (such as food causing salivation), is paired with a neutral stimulus (such as the sound of a bell). After repeated pairings, the neutral stimulus becomes a conditioned stimulus (CS) that triggers a similar response, now termed the conditioned response (CR). For example, in Pavlov's famous experiment, the sound of a bell (CS) was associated with the presentation of food (US), leading to dogs salivating (CR) in response to the bell alone.\n\nThis type of learning is often unconscious and automatic, forming the basis for many reflexive or involuntary behaviors, including emotional reactions and certain phobias. Classical conditioning illustrates how experiences can shape responses to stimuli, highlighting the associative nature of learning.

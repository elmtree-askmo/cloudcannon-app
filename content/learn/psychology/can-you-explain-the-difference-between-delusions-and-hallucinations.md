question: >-
    Can you explain the difference between delusions and hallucinations?
answer: >-
    Delusions and hallucinations are both symptoms of psychosis, but they represent different types of experiences.\n\n**Delusions** are fixed false beliefs that persist despite evidence to the contrary. They are cognitive in nature and involve a person's thoughts and beliefs. For example, an individual may believe they are being persecuted or that they have extraordinary abilities, even when there is no evidence to support these beliefs. Delusions can encompass a wide range of themes, including paranoia, grandiosity, and reference.\n\n**Hallucinations**, on the other hand, involve sensory perceptions that occur without any external stimuli. This means that a person may see, hear, smell, taste, or feel things that are not actually present in the environment. The most common type of hallucination in schizophrenia is auditory hallucinations, where individuals hear voices or sounds that do not exist.\n\nIn summary, the key difference is that delusions are about beliefs and thoughts that feel real to the individual, while hallucinations are sensory experiences that do not correspond to reality. Both can significantly impact a person's perception of reality and are often present in various mental health disorders, including schizophrenia.

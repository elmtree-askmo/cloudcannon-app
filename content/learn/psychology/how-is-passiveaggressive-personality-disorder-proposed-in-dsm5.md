question: >-
    How is Passive-Aggressive Personality Disorder proposed in DSM-5?
answer: >-
    Passive-Aggressive Personality Disorder (PAPD) is characterized by a pervasive pattern of negativistic attitudes and passive resistance to the demands for adequate performance in social and occupational situations. Although it was previously recognized in earlier editions of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM), such as the DSM-IV-TR, it is important to note that PAPD is not included as an official diagnosis in the DSM-5.\n\nIn the DSM-5, individuals exhibiting symptoms consistent with PAPD may be classified under "Other Specified Personality Disorder" or "Unspecified Personality Disorder." This classification is used when an individual meets the general criteria for a personality disorder but does not fit neatly into any specific category recognized by the DSM-5. The symptoms of PAPD include indirect expressions of displeasure, excessive suppression of emotions, and behaviors such as procrastination and subtle resistance to authority.\n\nThe prevalence of PAPD is estimated to be around 3.3%, but the exact etiology remains unclear due to limited research. It is suggested that a combination of genetic and environmental factors may contribute to the development of this disorder.\n\nThe DSM-5 acknowledges the complexity of diagnosing personality disorders, and the lack of specific diagnostic criteria for PAPD reflects the ongoing debate and research surrounding its construct validity. The psychologist Theodore Millon has proposed subtypes of "negativist" behavior, which may help in understanding the various manifestations of passive-aggressive traits.\n\nIn summary, while Passive-Aggressive Personality Disorder is recognized in the context of personality disorders, it does not have a distinct classification in the DSM-5, and individuals displaying such behaviors may be diagnosed under broader categories.

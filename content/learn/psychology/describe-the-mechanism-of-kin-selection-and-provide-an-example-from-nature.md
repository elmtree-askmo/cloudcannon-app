question: >-
    Describe the mechanism of kin selection and provide an example from nature.
answer: >-
    Kin selection is a mechanism of natural selection that favors behaviors that enhance the reproductive success of an individual's relatives, even if such behaviors come at a cost to the individual's own reproductive success. This concept is crucial in understanding altruistic behaviors in animals, where individuals may sacrifice their own fitness to benefit their kin.\n\n### Mechanism of Kin Selection\n\nThe underlying principle of kin selection is that individuals share a portion of their genes with their relatives. By helping relatives reproduce, an individual can indirectly pass on its own genes. This is quantified through the concept of **inclusive fitness**, which considers both direct fitness (an individual's own offspring) and indirect fitness (the offspring of relatives).\n\nHamilton's Rule formalizes this relationship and is expressed as:\n\n$$\nrB > C\n$$\n\nWhere:\n- $ r $ = genetic relatedness (the probability that two individuals share a gene)\n- $ B $ = benefit to the recipient of the altruistic act\n- $ C $ = cost to the actor performing the altruistic act\n\nIf the benefits to the recipient, weighted by their relatedness, exceed the costs to the actor, then the altruistic behavior is favored by natural selection.\n\n### Example from Nature\n\nA classic example of kin selection is observed in **ground squirrels**. When a ground squirrel spots a predator, it may emit a loud alarm call to warn other squirrels. This behavior increases the risk of predation for the caller, as it draws attention to itself. However, the alarm call benefits nearby relatives (siblings or offspring) by alerting them to danger, thus increasing their chances of survival. The genetic relatedness among these squirrels means that by helping their kin survive, the caller is indirectly ensuring the continuation of shared genes.\n\nAnother prominent example is found in **eusocial insects** like bees and ants. Worker bees, which are often sterile, will forgo their own reproduction to assist the queen in raising her offspring. Since the workers are closely related to the queen's offspring, their efforts help propagate their shared genetic material, demonstrating kin selection in action.\n\nIn summary, kin selection explains how altruistic behaviors can evolve in populations where individuals prioritize the reproductive success of their relatives, thereby enhancing their inclusive fitness.

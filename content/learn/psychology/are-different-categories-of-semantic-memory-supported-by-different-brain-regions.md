question: >-
    Are different categories of semantic memory supported by different brain regions?
answer: >-
    Yes, different categories of semantic memory are supported by different brain regions. Research indicates that semantic memory consists of both modality-specific and supramodal representations. The supramodal representations are supported by the gradual convergence of information throughout large regions of the temporal and inferior parietal association cortex. In contrast, modality-specific systems are associated with distinct neural encodings for category-based and feature-based semantic knowledge, which exist in various sensory, motor, lexical-semantic, and limbic domains.\n\nFunctional neuroimaging studies have shown that different categories of objects elicit distinct patterns of neural activity in specific brain regions. For example, processing action-related language activates brain regions involved in executing and planning actions. Additionally, the left prefrontal cortex, parietal, and posterior temporal lobes are critical for semantic memory, with specific functions and processes being subserved by smaller regions within these areas.\n\nOverall, the evidence suggests a complex interplay between various brain regions, where both modality-specific and abstract representations contribute to the organization and retrieval of semantic memory. This highlights the importance of understanding the neural networks involved in semantic processing and their implications for cognitive function and neurological disorders.

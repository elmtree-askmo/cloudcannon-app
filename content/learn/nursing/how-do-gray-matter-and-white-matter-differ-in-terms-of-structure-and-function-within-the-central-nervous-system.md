question: >-
    How do gray matter and white matter differ in terms of structure and function within the central nervous system?
answer: >-
    Gray matter and white matter are two fundamental components of the central nervous system (CNS), each with distinct structures and functions.\n\n### Structure\n\n- **Gray Matter**: \n  - Composed primarily of neuronal cell bodies, dendrites, and unmyelinated axons. \n  - It is found in the brain's cortex (the outer layer) and in deeper structures, as well as forming an 'H' shape in the center of the spinal cord.\n  - The gray matter is where most of the synaptic processing occurs, as it contains the majority of the neurons' cell bodies.\n\n- **White Matter**: \n  - Made up mainly of myelinated axons that connect different regions of gray matter. \n  - The myelin sheath surrounding these axons gives white matter its characteristic color and facilitates the rapid transmission of electrical signals between neurons.\n  - White matter is located beneath the gray matter in the brain and surrounds the gray matter in the spinal cord.\n\n### Function\n\n- **Gray Matter**: \n  - Involved in processing and integrating information. It plays a crucial role in functions such as muscle control, sensory perception, memory, emotions, and decision-making.\n  - The concentration of neuronal cell bodies in gray matter allows for complex processing and the formation of neural circuits.\n\n- **White Matter**: \n  - Primarily responsible for communication within the nervous system. It enables the transmission of signals between different brain regions and between the brain and spinal cord.\n  - The myelination of axons in white matter enhances the speed and efficiency of electrical signal conduction, which is essential for coordinated responses and overall brain function.\n\nIn summary, gray matter is primarily involved in processing and integrating information due to its high concentration of neuronal cell bodies, while white matter is crucial for communication between different parts of the CNS, facilitating rapid signal transmission through myelinated axons.

question: >-
    How is capital expenditure treated for tax purposes?
answer: >-
    In Zambia, capital expenditure is treated differently from regular business expenses for tax purposes. According to the Income Tax Act (Chapter 323), capital expenditures are generally not deductible when calculating taxable profits. This means that costs associated with acquiring, constructing, or improving fixed assets, such as buildings, machinery, and equipment, cannot be directly deducted from income.\n\nHowever, Zambia has a system of capital allowances that allows businesses to claim deductions for the wear and tear of capital assets over time. Specifically, businesses can claim an initial allowance of 10% of the cost incurred on an industrial building in the charge year in which the building is first brought into use. This initial allowance is a form of tax relief that recognizes the depreciation of the asset's value as it is used in the business.\n\nAdditionally, capital allowances are also available for capital expenditures related to mining operations and improvements in the farming industry. These allowances are designed to incentivize investment in capital assets by allowing businesses to recover some of their costs over time through tax deductions.\n\nIt is important for sole traders and businesses to differentiate between capital and revenue expenditures, as only the latter can be fully deducted in the year they are incurred. Capital expenditures, while not immediately deductible, can still provide tax benefits through the capital allowance system, which helps to reduce taxable income over the life of the asset. \n\nIn summary, while capital expenditures are not directly deductible, they can be accounted for through capital allowances, which provide a structured way to recover costs associated with long-term investments in business assets.

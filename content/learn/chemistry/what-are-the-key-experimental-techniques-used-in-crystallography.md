question: >-
    What are the key experimental techniques used in crystallography?
answer: >-
    The key experimental techniques used in crystallography include:\n\n1. **X-ray Crystallography**: This is the most widely used method for determining the atomic structure of crystals. It involves directing X-rays at a crystal, which diffract into specific patterns based on the arrangement of atoms within the crystal. The resulting diffraction pattern is analyzed to extract information about the crystal structure. There are two main types of X-ray diffraction:\n   - **Single-Crystal X-ray Diffraction**: This technique is used when a large enough single crystal is available. The crystal is rotated in the X-ray beam, and the scattered X-rays are recorded to determine the complete three-dimensional structure.\n   - **X-ray Powder Diffraction (XRPD)**: This method is used for polycrystalline samples where individual crystals are not large enough. XRPD provides less detailed information than single-crystal diffraction but is simpler and faster.\n\n2. **Neutron Crystallography**: This technique complements X-ray crystallography by using neutrons instead of X-rays. Neutrons are sensitive to the positions of nuclei and can provide information about light atoms, such as hydrogen, which are often difficult to detect with X-rays. Neutron crystallography is particularly useful for refining structures obtained from X-ray data.\n\n3. **Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy**: While not a crystallographic technique per se, NMR can provide structural information about molecules in solution and is often used in conjunction with crystallographic methods to gain insights into molecular dynamics and interactions.\n\n4. **Electron Microscopy**: This technique is used to visualize the structure of crystals at high resolutions. It can be particularly useful for studying biological macromolecules and complex structures that are difficult to crystallize.\n\n5. **Small Angle X-ray Scattering (SAXS)**: SAXS is used to study the size and shape of particles in solution, providing information about the overall structure of macromolecules and complexes.\n\n6. **Mass Spectrometry**: This technique can be used to analyze the mass and composition of molecules, which can aid in the identification of crystal structures.\n\n7. **Cryo-Electron Microscopy (Cryo-EM)**: This is a rapidly growing technique that allows for the visualization of biological macromolecules in their native state without the need for crystallization.\n\n8. **Chemical Crosslinking and Forster Resonance Energy Transfer (FRET)**: These methods are used to study interactions and conformational changes in proteins, providing additional context to crystallographic data.\n\nThese techniques are often used in combination to provide a comprehensive understanding of crystal structures and their properties, particularly in the fields of materials science and structural biology.

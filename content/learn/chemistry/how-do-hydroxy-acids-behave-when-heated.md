question: >-
    How do β-hydroxy acids behave when heated?
answer: >-
    When β-hydroxy acids are heated, particularly in the presence of strong mineral acids, they undergo a dehydration reaction that results in the formation of α,β-unsaturated acids. This process is analogous to the dehydration of alcohols to form alkenes. The mechanism of this reaction is largely self-catalyzed, meaning that the heat can facilitate the protonation of the hydroxy group, leading to the formation of a carbocation. Subsequently, water is eliminated, and a proton is abstracted from the α-position, resulting in the formation of an olefin.\n\nIn addition to this, γ-hydroxy acids, when subjected to heat, can undergo intramolecular esterification, producing a five-membered cyclic ester. This behavior highlights the distinct thermal reactions of hydroxy acids based on their structural classification.\n\nOverall, the thermal behavior of β-hydroxy acids is characterized by their ability to form unsaturated compounds through dehydration, which is a significant reaction in organic chemistry.

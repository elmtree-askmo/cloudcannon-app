question: >-
    Where is the place of delivery typically determined in a contract of sale?
answer: >-
    In a contract of sale, the place of delivery is typically determined by the terms agreed upon by the parties involved. According to the Sale of Goods Act, the parties are free to specify the place of delivery in their contract. If the contract does not explicitly state the place of delivery, the default rules apply:\n\n1. **Seller's Place of Business**: If the seller has a place of business, the place of delivery is generally considered to be the seller's place of business at the time of the sale.\n2. **Seller's Residence**: If the seller does not have a place of business, the place of delivery defaults to the seller's residence.\n3. **Specific Goods**: If the contract involves specific goods that both parties are aware of being located at a different place at the time of contracting, that location becomes the place of delivery.\n\nFor example, if a buyer in Atlanta enters into a sales contract with a seller in Chicago and the contract does not specify a place for delivery, the place of delivery would typically be the seller's place of business in Chicago.\n\nAdditionally, Section 36(1) of the Sale of Goods Act emphasizes that the parties can determine the location of delivery, and the seller must deliver the goods at that specified place during business hours on a working day. This flexibility allows for clarity and mutual agreement on the logistics of the transaction.

question: >-
    How does the UCC compare with the International Sale of Goods?
answer: >-
    The Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) and the United Nations Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG) serve similar purposes in governing the sale of goods, but they differ significantly in their application, principles, and specific provisions.\n\n1. **Scope and Application**:\n   - The UCC applies to the sale of goods within the United States and is designed to provide a uniform set of laws across all states. It was established in 1952 to simplify and standardize commercial transactions domestically.\n   - The CISG, on the other hand, is an international treaty that automatically applies to contracts for the sale of goods between parties whose principal places of business are in different countries that are signatories to the CISG. It has been ratified by 81 countries, including major trading nations.\n\n2. **Contract Formation**:\n   - Under the UCC, the "mirror-image rule" applies, meaning that an acceptance must exactly match the terms of the offer for a contract to be formed. Any deviation constitutes a counteroffer.\n   - The CISG is more flexible regarding contract formation. It allows for a contract to be formed even if the acceptance contains additional or different terms, provided that these do not materially alter the offer.\n\n3. **Warranties and Non-Conforming Goods**:\n   - The UCC has specific provisions regarding warranties, including implied warranties of merchantability and fitness for a particular purpose. These can be excluded by clear language, such as "as is."\n   - The CISG also addresses warranties but is generally more flexible regarding non-conforming goods. Under the CISG, a seller is not required to provide a "perfect tender," which is a requirement under the UCC. Instead, the CISG allows for a degree of non-conformity, provided it does not substantially deprive the buyer of what they were entitled to expect under the contract.\n\n4. **Statute of Frauds**:\n   - The UCC includes a statute of frauds provision that requires certain contracts to be in writing to be enforceable, specifically those involving goods priced at $500 or more.\n   - The CISG does not have a similar requirement for written contracts, which can lead to different interpretations and enforcement of agreements.\n\n5. **Legal Framework**:\n   - The UCC is rooted in American common law and reflects the legal traditions and practices of the United States.\n   - The CISG was developed as a compromise between common law and civil law systems, aiming to facilitate international trade by providing a neutral legal framework that accommodates various legal traditions.\n\nIn summary, while both the UCC and CISG aim to regulate the sale of goods, they do so in different contexts and with different rules. Understanding these differences is crucial for parties engaging in domestic versus international sales transactions to ensure compliance with the appropriate legal framework.

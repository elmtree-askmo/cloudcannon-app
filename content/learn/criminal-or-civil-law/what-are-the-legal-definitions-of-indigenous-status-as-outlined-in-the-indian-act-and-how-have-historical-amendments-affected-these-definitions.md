question: >-
    What are the legal definitions of indigenous status as outlined in the Indian Act, and how have historical amendments affected these definitions?
answer: >-
    The legal definitions of Indigenous status in Canada are primarily outlined in the Indian Act, which was enacted in 1876. This Act has historically served as a framework for defining who is considered Indigenous, specifically focusing on "Status Indians," which refers to individuals recognized by the federal government as belonging to a First Nation. The Act includes provisions that dictate the criteria for obtaining and maintaining this status, which can significantly impact access to rights, resources, and recognition as Indigenous peoples.\n\n### Key Aspects of the Indian Act and Legal Definitions:\n\n1. **Definition of Status**: The Indian Act defines a "Status Indian" as a person who is registered on the Indian Register. This registration is based on descent from a person who was registered under the Act or who meets specific criteria set forth in the legislation.\n\n2. **Amendments**: Over the years, the Indian Act has undergone several amendments that have affected the definitions of Indigenous status:\n   - **Bill C-31 (1985)**: This amendment was significant as it aimed to address gender discrimination in the Act. Prior to this amendment, women who married non-Indigenous men lost their status, while men who married non-Indigenous women did not. Bill C-31 allowed women who lost their status to regain it and also provided a pathway for their children to obtain status.\n   - **Bill S-3 (2017)**: This amendment further addressed issues of gender discrimination and aimed to eliminate remaining inequalities in the registration process. It allowed individuals who were previously denied status due to discriminatory provisions to apply for registration.\n\n3. **Impact of Colonialism**: The Indian Act and its definitions of Indigenous status reflect the broader context of colonialism in Canada. The Act has been criticized for imposing external definitions of identity that can create divisions within Indigenous communities and complicate self-identification. The complexities of legal status often lead to disputes over identity and authenticity, as the Act's definitions do not encompass the full diversity of Indigenous identities and experiences.\n\n4. **Self-Definition and Governance**: The ongoing control exerted by the Indian Act over Indigenous identity has led to calls for greater self-definition and governance among Indigenous peoples. Many communities advocate for the right to define their own identities and statuses, independent of government-imposed frameworks.\n\n### Conclusion\n\nThe legal definitions of Indigenous status as outlined in the Indian Act are complex and have evolved through various amendments aimed at addressing historical injustices. However, the legacy of colonialism continues to influence these definitions, highlighting the importance of Indigenous self-definition and governance in the ongoing struggle for recognition and rights. The amendments, while progressive in some respects, still reflect a system that many Indigenous peoples seek to reform or replace with frameworks that honor their autonomy and cultural integrity.

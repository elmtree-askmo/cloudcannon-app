question: >-
    What is Acute Dystonia and how is it managed in patients taking antipsychotics?
answer: >-
    Acute dystonia is a movement disorder characterized by involuntary muscle contractions, which can lead to repetitive or twisting movements and abnormal postures. It is a common side effect associated with the use of antipsychotic medications, particularly typical antipsychotics, and can occur shortly after the initiation of treatment. The prevalence of antipsychotic-induced movement disorders, including acute dystonia, is estimated to be around 37%, with acute dystonic reactions occurring in up to 33% of acutely psychotic patients within the first few days of treatment.\n\nManagement of acute dystonia typically involves the use of anticholinergic agents, such as benztropine, or antihistamines like diphenhydramine. These medications work by blocking the action of acetylcholine, which helps to alleviate the involuntary movements associated with dystonia. In some cases, benzodiazepines, such as diazepam, may also be administered to help manage symptoms. These medications are often given intravenously or intramuscularly, especially since patients experiencing acute dystonia may have difficulty swallowing.\n\nClose follow-up with psychiatric care is essential for patients who experience acute dystonic reactions, as ongoing communication among healthcare team members is crucial for early recognition and effective treatment. While there is currently no cure for dystonia, the focus of treatment is on alleviating symptoms to improve the patient's quality of life. \n\nIn summary, acute dystonia is a significant side effect of antipsychotic medications that requires prompt recognition and management to ensure patient safety and adherence to treatment.

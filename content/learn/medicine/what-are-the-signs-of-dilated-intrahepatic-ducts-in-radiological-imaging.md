question: >-
    What are the signs of dilated intrahepatic ducts in radiological imaging?
answer: >-
    In radiological imaging, signs of dilated intrahepatic ducts can be identified through various imaging modalities, including ultrasound, CT scans, and MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography). Here are some key signs and features associated with dilated intrahepatic ducts:\n\n1. **Central Dot Sign**: This is a significant indicator of dilated intrahepatic biliary ducts (IBD). It appears as a punctate dot of contrast enhancement within the dilated ducts, representing the enhancing branches of the portal vein surrounded by the dilated bile ducts.\n\n2. **Ductal Dilatation Measurements**: Intrahepatic ductal dilatation is defined as a ductal diameter greater than 2 mm. For extrahepatic ducts, dilatation of the common hepatic or bile duct is noted when it exceeds 6-7 mm or more than 40% of the diameter of the adjacent portal vein.\n\n3. **Distribution Patterns**: Most commonly, intrahepatic duct dilatation is segmental (83% of cases), while diffuse dilatation is less common (17%). The shape of the dilatation can be saccular in 76% of cases or fusiform in 24%.\n\n4. **Ultrasound Findings**: On ultrasound, dilated intrahepatic ducts may appear as tubular anechoic fluid-filled structures accompanying portal veins. The "shotgun sign" may also be observed, where the intrahepatic biliary ducts become tortuous and exceed 2 mm in diameter.\n\n5. **CT Imaging Features**: CT scans can reveal dilated bile ducts and help identify underlying causes such as tumors or gallstones. Key features include the location and degree of dilatation, attenuation within the dilated bile duct, and associated findings like intraductal masses.\n\n6. **Doppler Ultrasound**: Doppler ultrasound can be used to assess blood flow in the portal vein and hepatic artery, helping to differentiate between bile ducts and blood vessels, which can be crucial in cases of obstruction.\n\nThese signs and imaging characteristics are essential for diagnosing and understanding the underlying causes of intrahepatic biliary dilatation, which can result from various conditions such as obstructions, tumors, or infections.

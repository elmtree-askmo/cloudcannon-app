question: >-
    How do keratotic lesions such as seborrheic keratosis and actinic keratosis differ?
answer: >-
    Keratotic lesions, specifically seborrheic keratosis (SK) and actinic keratosis (AK), are two distinct types of skin growths that differ significantly in their characteristics, causes, and potential risks.\n\n1. **Causes**:\n   - **Actinic Keratosis**: Primarily caused by prolonged exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun. This condition is often seen in individuals with fair skin and is more common in areas of the skin that are frequently exposed to sunlight.\n   - **Seborrheic Keratosis**: Generally considered to be genetically determined and is not directly related to sun exposure. It is a common benign skin neoplasm that can occur in various individuals, regardless of sun exposure history.\n\n2. **Appearance and Texture**:\n   - **Actinic Keratosis**: Typically presents as rough, scaly patches or lesions that can be pink, red, or flesh-colored. These lesions are often flat or slightly raised and tend to be less pigmented than seborrheic keratosis. They usually appear in sun-exposed areas of the skin.\n   - **Seborrheic Keratosis**: These lesions are usually raised, have a round or oval shape, and can vary in color from light tan to black. They often have a smooth, shiny surface and a characteristic "stuck-on" appearance.\n\n3. **Risk Profile**:\n   - **Actinic Keratosis**: Carries a risk of progression to skin cancer, with estimates suggesting that about 5% to 10% of actinic keratoses may develop into squamous cell carcinoma if left untreated. Therefore, they are considered pre-malignant lesions.\n   - **Seborrheic Keratosis**: These lesions are benign and do not pose a cancer risk. They are primarily of aesthetic concern and may be treated if they become bothersome or irritating.\n\n4. **Management**:\n   - **Actinic Keratosis**: Treatment options may include topical therapies, cryotherapy, or photodynamic therapy, aimed at preventing progression to skin cancer.\n   - **Seborrheic Keratosis**: Treatment is usually not necessary unless the lesions are symptomatic or for cosmetic reasons. Options include cryotherapy, curettage, or laser therapy.\n\nIn summary, while both actinic keratosis and seborrheic keratosis are keratotic lesions, they differ significantly in their causes, appearance, risk of malignancy, and management strategies. Understanding these differences is crucial for proper diagnosis and treatment.

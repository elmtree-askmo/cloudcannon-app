question: >-
    What is the function of α1-antitrypsin in liver health?
answer: >-
    Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) is a crucial serine protease inhibitor primarily produced in the liver. Its primary function is to inhibit the activity of neutrophil elastase, an enzyme that can cause proteolytic damage in the lungs. By doing so, AAT plays a significant role in protecting lung tissue from inflammation and damage, particularly in conditions where neutrophils are activated, such as infections or inflammatory diseases.\n\nIn terms of liver health, AAT is vital because its deficiency can lead to significant liver damage. The most common form of deficiency, known as alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (AATD), is caused by mutations in the SERPINA1 gene. This results in the production of a misfolded variant of AAT, often referred to as the Z variant. When this variant accumulates in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) of hepatocytes (liver cells), it can lead to a toxic gain-of-function effect, causing chronic liver disease. The accumulation triggers a proinflammatory response and can lead to fibrosis and cirrhosis over time.\n\nIndividuals with AATD may present with liver dysfunction at various ages, from infancy to adulthood, and are at increased risk for developing conditions such as cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma. The liver damage associated with AATD is particularly concerning because it can occur independently of lung disease, although many patients may experience both.\n\nIn summary, α1-antitrypsin is essential for maintaining lung health, and its deficiency can have serious implications for liver health, leading to chronic liver disease and other complications. Understanding the role of AAT in liver function is crucial for diagnosing and managing conditions related to AATD.

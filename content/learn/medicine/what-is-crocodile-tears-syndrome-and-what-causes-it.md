question: >-
    What is Crocodile Tears Syndrome and what causes it?
answer: >-
    Crocodile Tears Syndrome, also known as Gustatory Lacrimation or Bogorad Syndrome, is a rare condition characterized by the involuntary production of tears while eating, drinking, or even smelling food. This phenomenon is often linked to facial nerve damage, particularly following conditions such as Bell's palsy, trauma, or surgical procedures affecting the facial nerve.\n\n### Causes\nThe primary cause of Crocodile Tears Syndrome is damage to the facial nerve (cranial nerve VII). This damage can occur due to various factors, including:\n- **Trauma**: Physical injury to the face can disrupt the normal functioning of the facial nerve.\n- **Surgical Procedures**: Surgeries involving the parotid gland or facial nerve decompression can lead to nerve injury.\n- **Medical Conditions**: Conditions such as Bell's palsy, stroke, or infections can also result in facial nerve damage.\n\nDuring the healing process, the nerve fibers may regenerate abnormally, leading to the misrouting of nerve signals. This misrouting causes the salivary glands to stimulate the lacrimal glands (which produce tears) when the individual eats or smells food, resulting in excessive tearing.\n\n### Symptoms\nThe hallmark symptom of Crocodile Tears Syndrome is excessive tearing during meals, which can occur alongside other symptoms such as:\n- **Hypersalivation**: Increased saliva production while eating.\n- **Flushing or Sweating**: Sweating on the affected side of the face.\n- **Photophobia**: Sensitivity to light.\n\n### Diagnosis and Management\nDiagnosis of Crocodile Tears Syndrome is often a diagnosis of exclusion, typically made in patients with a history of facial nerve palsy. A latency period of about six months is usually observed between the onset of facial nerve injury and the development of the syndrome, allowing time for nerve regeneration.\n\nManagement strategies may include:\n- **Symptomatic Treatment**: Addressing the symptoms as they arise, such as using eye drops to manage excessive tearing.\n- **Botulinum Toxin Injections**: In some cases, injections may be used to alleviate symptoms, although this is not a cure and may require repeat treatments.\n\nCrocodile Tears Syndrome is a fascinating example of how complex and interconnected the nervous system is, illustrating the unexpected consequences of nerve damage and regeneration.

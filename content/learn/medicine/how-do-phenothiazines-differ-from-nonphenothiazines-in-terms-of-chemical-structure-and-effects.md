question: >-
    How do phenothiazines differ from nonphenothiazines in terms of chemical structure and effects?
answer: >-
    Phenothiazines and nonphenothiazines are both classes of first-generation antipsychotic medications, but they differ significantly in their chemical structure and effects.\n\n### Chemical Structure\n- **Phenothiazines**: This class is characterized by a tricyclic structure that includes a phenothiazine core, which has the general formula S(C₆H₄)₂NH. The structure allows for various substitutions at the C-2 position, leading to different derivatives with distinct pharmacological properties. Common examples include chlorpromazine and promethazine.\n  \n- **Nonphenothiazines**: This group includes several different classes of antipsychotics, such as butyrophenones (e.g., haloperidol), dibenzoxapines, dihydroindolones, and thioxanthenes. These compounds do not share the phenothiazine core structure but may have similar mechanisms of action, primarily through dopamine receptor antagonism.\n\n### Effects\n- **Phenothiazines**: They exhibit a broad spectrum of effects due to their ability to antagonize multiple neurotransmitter receptors, including dopamine, serotonin, histamine, and α-adrenergic receptors. This results in a diverse side effect profile, which can include sedation, anticholinergic effects (such as blurred vision and dry mouth), and extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) like pseudoparkinsonism, akathisia, and tardive dyskinesia.\n\n- **Nonphenothiazines**: While they also primarily act as dopamine antagonists, nonphenothiazines tend to have a different side effect profile. For instance, butyrophenones are known for a higher incidence of EPS compared to some phenothiazines, particularly those with a piperazine side chain. Nonphenothiazines may also be less sedating than certain phenothiazines, depending on their specific chemical structure.\n\nIn summary, the primary differences between phenothiazines and nonphenothiazines lie in their chemical structures and the resulting pharmacological effects, including their side effect profiles and receptor interactions.

question: >-
    How is the post-truth era defined and what are its main characteristics?
answer: >-
    The term "post-truth" refers to a cultural and political context in which objective facts are less influential in shaping public opinion than appeals to emotion and personal belief. This concept gained prominence in the mid-2010s, particularly during the 2016 United States presidential elections, and was notably recognized by Oxford Dictionaries as the word of the year in 2016.\n\n### Main Characteristics of the Post-Truth Era:\n\n1. **Emotional Appeals Over Facts**: In a post-truth environment, individuals are more likely to accept information that resonates with their emotions or personal beliefs rather than information that is factually accurate. This shift indicates a prioritization of subjective experience over objective reality.\n\n2. **Misinformation and Fake News**: The proliferation of misinformation and "fake news" has become a hallmark of the post-truth era. This phenomenon is exacerbated by social media platforms, which facilitate the rapid spread of false information, often leading to public confusion and mistrust in traditional news sources.\n\n3. **Public Anxiety About Truth**: There is a growing public anxiety regarding the legitimacy of truth claims and the authority of those who present themselves as truth-tellers. This skepticism can lead to a general distrust of institutions, including the media and government.\n\n4. **Partisanship and Polarization**: The post-truth era is characterized by heightened partisanship, where individuals are more likely to condone misinformation that aligns with their political beliefs. This polarization can create echo chambers, where people are exposed primarily to information that reinforces their existing views.\n\n5. **Relativity of Truth**: The post-truth context is often linked to post-modernist assumptions about the relativity of truth. This philosophical perspective challenges the idea of an absolute truth, suggesting that truth can be subjective and context-dependent.\n\n6. **Psychological Mechanisms**: Psychological factors such as imagination, repetition, and partisanship play significant roles in how misinformation is accepted and perpetuated. These mechanisms can lead individuals to not only believe false information but also to dismiss factual corrections.\n\n7. **Impact on Democracy**: The post-truth era poses significant challenges to democratic processes, as misinformation can shape political attitudes and influence electoral outcomes. The erosion of a shared factual basis for public discourse undermines informed decision-making.\n\nIn summary, the post-truth era is defined by a complex interplay of emotional reasoning, misinformation, public distrust, and philosophical debates about the nature of truth. Understanding these characteristics is crucial for addressing the challenges posed by misinformation and fostering a more informed public discourse.

question: >-
    How is cultural change defined and what are its main characteristics?
answer: >-
    Cultural change is defined as the dynamic transformation of shared practices, beliefs, and behaviors within a community or society over time. It reflects alterations in the collective mindset, often in response to various factors such as environmental shifts, technological advancements, or social dynamics. \n\n### Main Characteristics of Cultural Change:\n\n1. **Dynamic Nature**: Culture is not static; it is always evolving. New objects and ideas are continuously introduced, which can significantly alter both material and nonmaterial aspects of culture. For instance, the introduction of smartphones has changed communication practices and social interactions.\n\n2. **Adaptability**: Culture must be adaptive to survive. This means that cultural change is a continuous process that accommodates technological innovations and cultural diffusion. For example, the way people clean their teeth has evolved with the introduction of new dental products and practices.\n\n3. **Shared Practices**: Cultural change involves shifts in the behaviors and norms that are shared among members of a society. These changes often require individuals to act in socially appropriate ways that align with the evolving culture.\n\n4. **Symbolism**: Culture is based on symbols, which can vary across different societies. Changes in cultural symbols can lead to significant shifts in societal beliefs and practices.\n\n5. **Interconnectedness**: Culture is an integrated system; changes in one area can lead to changes in others. For example, advancements in technology can influence social norms, economic practices, and even political structures.\n\n6. **Innovation and Evolution**: Innovation serves as a driving force behind cultural change, altering traditions and mindsets. Evolutionary changes may involve modifications in cultural norms, technologies, or beliefs, demonstrating the potential for future growth and transformation.\n\n7. **Impact on Society**: Cultural change can bring issues of intergroup relations, prejudice, and identity to the forefront. It can also lead to cultural gaps, where material culture may diffuse rapidly while nonmaterial culture, such as beliefs and values, may take longer to adapt.\n\nIn summary, cultural change is a complex and multifaceted process that reflects the ongoing evolution of societal norms, practices, and beliefs in response to various internal and external influences.

question: >-
    How did the sinking of the Lusitania influence American attitudes towards the war?
answer: >-
    The sinking of the RMS Lusitania on May 7, 1915, had a profound impact on American attitudes towards World War I, significantly shifting public opinion against Germany and in favor of joining the Allied war effort. The Lusitania, a British ocean liner, was torpedoed by a German U-boat, resulting in the deaths of 1,198 people, including 128 American citizens. This tragic event was one of the most dramatic incidents that polarized American sentiment during the war.\n\nIn the immediate aftermath, American newspapers across the political spectrum labeled the sinking as one of the most serious events of the war for the United States. Publications like the Harrisburg Telegraph and The New York Times highlighted the tragedy, emphasizing the loss of American lives and the implications for U.S. neutrality. The media's coverage played a crucial role in shaping public perception, as they scoured the lists of survivors and the missing, further amplifying the emotional response.\n\nThe German government's reaction to the sinking, which included defiance and even jubilation, further inflamed American anger. This sentiment was compounded by the perception that Germany was waging war indiscriminately, targeting civilians as well as military personnel. The Lusitania incident thus became a rallying point for those advocating for U.S. involvement in the war.\n\nWhile the United States did not enter the war immediately following the Lusitania's sinking, the event laid the groundwork for a shift in public opinion that would eventually lead to U.S. involvement. It marked a turning point in American neutrality, as the outrage over the loss of civilian lives contributed to a growing sentiment that the U.S. could no longer remain uninvolved in the conflict. The Lusitania's sinking is often cited as a key factor that hastened the United States' entry into World War I, which would ultimately prove decisive for the Allies' victory.

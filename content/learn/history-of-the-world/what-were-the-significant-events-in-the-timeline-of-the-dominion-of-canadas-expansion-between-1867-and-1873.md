question: >-
    What were the significant events in the timeline of the Dominion of Canada's expansion between 1867 and 1873?
answer: >-
    Between 1867 and 1873, several significant events marked the expansion of the Dominion of Canada. Here is a timeline of these key events:\n\n1. **1867 - Confederation**: The Dominion of Canada was officially established on July 1, 1867, through the British North America Act. This act united the provinces of Ontario, Quebec, New Brunswick, and Nova Scotia into a self-governing entity within the British Empire.\n\n2. **1870 - Manitoba Joins Canada**: In 1870, Manitoba was created as a province, following the transfer of Rupert's Land and the North-Western Territory from the Hudson's Bay Company to Canada. This was part of the broader process of expanding Canadian territory and governance into the West.\n\n3. **1871 - British Columbia Joins Canada**: British Columbia became the sixth province of Canada in 1871. The decision to join Confederation was influenced by the promise of a transcontinental railway, which would connect the province to the rest of Canada.\n\n4. **1873 - Prince Edward Island Joins Canada**: On May 23, 1873, Prince Edward Island became the seventh province of Canada. The island had initially opted out of Confederation in 1867 but later joined due to economic pressures and the promise of financial assistance from the federal government.\n\n5. **Establishment of the North-West Mounted Police (1873)**: In 1873, the North-West Mounted Police (NWMP) were established, which would later become the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP). This force was created to maintain law and order in the newly acquired western territories and to assert Canadian sovereignty in the region.\n\nThese events were crucial in shaping the early years of the Dominion of Canada, facilitating its territorial expansion and the integration of diverse regions into a unified nation. The period also set the stage for future interactions with Indigenous peoples and the complexities of federal-provincial relations in Canada.

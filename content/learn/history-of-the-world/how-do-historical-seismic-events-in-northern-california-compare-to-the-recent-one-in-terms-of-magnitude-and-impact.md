question: >-
    How do historical seismic events in Northern California compare to the recent one in terms of magnitude and impact?
answer: >-
    Historical seismic events in Northern California provide a context for understanding the recent 7.0 magnitude earthquake that occurred off the coast. This recent event is significant as it marks the eighth earthquake of magnitude 7.0 or higher in the Northern California region in the past century. \n\nIn comparison, the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake, which had a magnitude of 6.9, resulted in 63 fatalities and approximately $6 billion in property damage. This highlights the potential for severe impact even with slightly lower magnitudes. The 1906 San Francisco earthquake, one of the most infamous in California's history, had a magnitude estimated between 7.8 and 8.3 and caused extensive destruction and loss of life.\n\nThe recent earthquake prompted a tsunami warning for about 5 million people along the West Coast, reflecting the ongoing risk of tsunamis associated with significant seismic activity. For instance, the 2011 tsunami from the Tohoku earthquake in Japan caused over $100 million in damage to California's coastal infrastructure, illustrating the far-reaching effects of seismic events.\n\nOverall, while the recent 7.0 magnitude earthquake is a notable event, it is part of a broader historical pattern of significant seismic activity in Northern California, with past events demonstrating the potential for both immediate and long-term impacts on communities and infrastructure.

question: >-
    What were the key terms of the Treaty of Versailles, and how did they contribute to public resentment in Germany?
answer: >-
    The Treaty of Versailles, signed on June 28, 1919, was a pivotal peace treaty that officially ended World War I. It imposed several key terms on Germany, which significantly contributed to public resentment and had lasting implications for the political landscape of Germany and Europe. Here are the main terms of the treaty and their effects:\n\n1. **War Guilt Clause**: Germany was required to accept full responsibility for the war under Article 231, commonly referred to as the War Guilt Clause. This clause was perceived as a national humiliation and fostered a sense of injustice among the German populace.\n\n2. **Territorial Losses**: Germany lost approximately 65,000 square kilometers of territory and around 7 million people. Key territorial changes included:\n   - The return of Alsace-Lorraine to France.\n   - The cession of territories to Belgium, France, Denmark, and Poland.\n   - The loss of all German colonies.\n   - The demilitarization of the Rhineland, which further restricted Germany's military capabilities.\n\n3. **Military Restrictions**: The treaty imposed severe limitations on the German military, including:\n   - A reduction of the army to 200,000 men.\n   - The prohibition of conscription.\n   - The ban on tanks, aircraft, and submarines, although Germany was still allowed to maintain a small air force.\n\n4. **Reparations**: Germany was required to pay reparations estimated at Â£100 million per year for 66 years. This financial burden crippled the German economy and contributed to hyperinflation and economic instability in the 1920s.\n\nThe combination of these terms led to widespread resentment among the German population. Many Germans viewed the treaty as a "Diktat" (dictated peace) that was imposed without their input, leading to feelings of humiliation and injustice. This resentment was a significant factor in the political landscape of post-war Germany, contributing to the rise of extremist parties, particularly the Nazis, who capitalized on public discontent by promising to overturn the treaty and restore Germany's former glory.\n\nIn summary, the Treaty of Versailles not only imposed harsh penalties on Germany but also created a legacy of resentment that played a crucial role in the rise of Adolf Hitler and the eventual outbreak of World War II. The treaty's terms were seen as a national humiliation, and the economic hardships it caused further fueled political instability in the Weimar Republic.

question: >-
    Who was the Young Pretender and how did he influence the Jacobite Rebellions?
answer: >-
    The Young Pretender, also known as Charles Edward Stuart or Bonnie Prince Charlie, was born on December 31, 1720, in Rome. He was the last serious claimant of the Stuart line to the British throne and is best known for leading the Jacobite rebellion of 1745-46, which aimed to restore his father, James Edward Stuart (the Old Pretender), to the throne.\n\nCharles's influence on the Jacobite Rebellions was significant, particularly during the 1745 uprising, often referred to as the '45. After failing to secure support from the French government for an invasion, he took matters into his own hands. He sailed from France to Scotland, landing on Eriskay in July 1745, and began to rally support among the Scottish Highlanders. His charisma and leadership allowed him to gather an army of approximately 10,000 men, capturing Edinburgh and achieving initial successes against the Hanoverian forces.\n\nHowever, the rebellion ultimately faced challenges, including a lack of resources and support, as well as strategic miscalculations. The campaign culminated in the disastrous Battle of Culloden on April 16, 1746, where Charles's forces were decisively defeated. Following this defeat, he fled to France, marking the end of the Jacobite Rebellion of 1745-46.\n\nCharles Edward Stuart remains a romanticized figure in Scottish history, symbolizing the Jacobite cause and the struggle against the Hanoverian rule. His legacy continues to resonate in Scottish culture, where he is often viewed as a national hero despite the failure of his rebellion.

question: >-
    What is Faraday's Law and how is it mathematically represented?
answer: >-
    Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction is a fundamental principle in electromagnetism that describes how a changing magnetic field can induce an electromotive force (emf) in a conductor. This law was formulated by the English scientist Michael Faraday in 1831 based on his experimental observations.\n\nThe law states that the induced emf in any closed circuit is equal to the negative rate of change of the magnetic flux through the circuit. Mathematically, this relationship can be expressed as:\n\n$$\n\text{emf} = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\n$$\n\nWhere:\n- **emf** is the electromotive force measured in volts (V),\n- $d\Phi$ is the change in magnetic flux measured in webers (Wb),\n- $dt$ is the change in time measured in seconds (s).\n\nThe negative sign in the equation is a reflection of Lenz's Law, which states that the direction of the induced emf will always be such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it. This principle is crucial in understanding how electric generators, transformers, and inductors operate.\n\nFaraday's Law not only establishes a quantitative relationship between changing magnetic fields and induced voltages but also lays the groundwork for many modern electrical technologies. It highlights the interconnectedness of electricity and magnetism, which was further developed by James Clerk Maxwell in his formulation of electromagnetic theory.

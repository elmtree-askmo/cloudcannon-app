question: >-
    What triggers the transition from a main-sequence star to a red giant?
answer: >-
    The transition from a main-sequence star to a red giant is primarily triggered by the exhaustion of hydrogen fuel in the star's core. During the main-sequence phase, stars like the Sun fuse hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion, which generates the energy that supports the star against gravitational collapse. This process can last for billions of years, depending on the star's mass.\n\nOnce the hydrogen in the core is depleted, the core contracts under gravity, leading to an increase in temperature and pressure. This contraction causes the outer layers of the star to expand significantly, resulting in the star becoming a red giant. The increase in luminosity during this phase is due to the gravitational contraction and the onset of hydrogen shell burning around the core, which moves the star up on the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram, while the expansion lowers the effective temperature, causing the star to move to the right on the diagram.\n\nIn summary, the key steps in the transition from a main-sequence star to a red giant are:\n\n1. Exhaustion of hydrogen fuel in the core.\n2. Core contraction and heating.\n3. Expansion of the outer layers, leading to increased luminosity and a decrease in surface temperature.\n\nThis evolutionary process is a common phase for low- and intermediate-mass stars, including our Sun, which is expected to enter the red giant phase in about four to five billion years.

question: >-
    How does the gravitational force between two masses depend on their mass and distance?
answer: >-
    The gravitational force between two masses depends on both their masses and the distance between them, as described by Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation. The relationship is mathematically expressed by the formula:\n\n$$\nF = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\n$$\n\nwhere:\n- $ F $ is the gravitational force,\n- $ G $ is the gravitational constant, approximately $ 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \text{N m}^2/\text{kg}^2 $,\n- $ m_1 $ and $ m_2 $ are the masses of the two objects,\n- $ r $ is the distance between the centers of the two masses.\n\n### Key Points:\n1. **Direct Proportionality to Masses**: The gravitational force is directly proportional to the product of the two masses. This means that if either mass increases, the gravitational force between them also increases. For example, if you double one of the masses, the gravitational force will also double.\n\n2. **Inverse Square Law**: The gravitational force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between the two masses. This means that as the distance $ r $ increases, the gravitational force decreases rapidly. Specifically, if the distance is doubled, the gravitational force becomes one-fourth as strong, since \( (2r)^2 = 4r^2 \).\n\n3. **Implications**: This relationship explains why celestial bodies, such as planets and moons, exert significant gravitational forces on each other, influencing their orbits and motions. It also highlights the diminishing effect of gravity over larger distances, which is why distant objects in space have a much weaker gravitational influence on each other compared to nearby objects.\n\nIn summary, the gravitational force is a fundamental interaction that varies with the masses involved and the distance separating them, following the principles outlined in Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation.

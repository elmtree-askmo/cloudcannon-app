question: >-
    What distinguishes reflecting telescopes from refracting telescopes?
answer: >-
    Reflecting telescopes and refracting telescopes are distinguished primarily by their optical components and the principles they use to gather and focus light.\n\n1. **Optical Components**:\n   - **Reflecting Telescopes**: These telescopes utilize mirrors to gather and focus light. They typically consist of a primary mirror that collects light and a secondary mirror that directs the light to the eyepiece or camera. The design allows for larger apertures, which can capture more light, making them effective for observing faint celestial objects.\n   - **Refracting Telescopes**: In contrast, refracting telescopes use lenses to bend and focus light. They rely on an objective lens to gather light and direct it through the telescope to the eyepiece. This design can lead to longer and heavier telescopes, especially as the size of the lenses increases.\n\n2. **Optical Principles**:\n   - **Reflecting Telescopes**: The principle behind these telescopes is the reflection of light off a curved mirror. This allows for the construction of larger telescopes without the issues of lens sagging or distortion that can affect refractors.\n   - **Refracting Telescopes**: These telescopes operate on the principle of refraction, where light bends as it passes through the lens. This can result in better contrast and sharpness for certain observations, but larger refractors can suffer from optical aberrations and distortion.\n\n3. **Cost and Maintenance**:\n   - Reflecting telescopes are generally more cost-effective to produce, especially for larger apertures, making them accessible for amateur astronomers. They also require less maintenance compared to refractors, which can be more complex due to their lens systems.\n   - Refracting telescopes, while often providing clearer images for specific types of observations (like deep space objects), can be more expensive and heavier, making them less portable.\n\n4. **Applications**:\n   - Reflectors are favored for observing larger and brighter objects, such as the Moon and planets, due to their ability to gather more light. \n   - Refractors are often preferred for viewing deep space objects like galaxies and nebulae, where clarity and contrast are crucial.\n\nIn summary, the main distinctions between reflecting and refracting telescopes lie in their use of mirrors versus lenses, the principles of light manipulation they employ, their cost-effectiveness, and their suitability for different types of astronomical observations.

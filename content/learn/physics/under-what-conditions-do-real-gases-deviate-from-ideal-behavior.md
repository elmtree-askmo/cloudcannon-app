question: >-
    Under what conditions do real gases deviate from ideal behavior?
answer: >-
    Real gases deviate from ideal behavior primarily under conditions of high pressure and low temperatures. Under these circumstances, the assumptions of the ideal gas law, which posits that gas molecules occupy no volume and experience no intermolecular forces, become increasingly inaccurate.\n\n1. **High Pressure**: At high pressures, the volume of the gas molecules becomes significant compared to the total volume of the gas. This leads to a situation where the actual volume occupied by the gas is greater than what is predicted by the ideal gas law. The measured pressure of the gas is often greater than what the ideal gas law would suggest.\n\n2. **Low Temperature**: At low temperatures, gas molecules have lower kinetic energy, which allows intermolecular forces (such as van der Waals forces) to become more significant. These attractive forces can lead to a decrease in pressure compared to what would be expected from the ideal gas law.\n\n3. **Critical Point**: The deviation from ideal behavior is most pronounced near the critical point of a substance, where the distinction between liquid and gas phases becomes less clear. \n\n4. **Empirical Corrections**: The van der Waals equation is one way to account for these deviations. It introduces constants that correct for the volume occupied by gas molecules and the attractive forces between them, providing a more accurate description of real gas behavior under various conditions.\n\nIn summary, real gases tend to behave more like ideal gases at high temperatures and low pressures, while significant deviations occur at low temperatures and high pressures, leading to phenomena such as condensation and changes in phase.

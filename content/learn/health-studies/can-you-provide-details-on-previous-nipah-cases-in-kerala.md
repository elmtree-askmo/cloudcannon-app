answer: >-
    The Nipah virus has been a significant public health concern in Kerala, India, with multiple outbreaks reported over the years. Here are the key details regarding previous Nipah cases in the state:\n\n1. **2018 Outbreak**: The first Nipah virus outbreak in South India occurred in May 2018, primarily affecting the Kozhikode and Malappuram districts. This outbreak resulted in 18 confirmed cases and 17 deaths by June 1, 2018. The outbreak was traced back to fruit bats, which are known carriers of the virus. It was declared over on June 10, 2018.\n\n2. **2019 Outbreak**: A subsequent outbreak occurred in June 2019 in the Ernakulam district. A 21-year-old male student was diagnosed with Nipah virus infection, confirmed through RT-PCR testing. Fortunately, this outbreak did not result in any fatalities, showcasing the effectiveness of the public health response based on lessons learned from the previous outbreak.\n\n3. **2021 Outbreak**: Another outbreak was reported in 2021, although specific details regarding the number of cases and fatalities were not highlighted in the gathered information.\n\n4. **2023 Outbreak**: The most recent outbreak occurred in late August 2023, with six laboratory-confirmed cases reported, including two deaths in September 2023. This resurgence of the virus has prompted public health authorities to implement stringent measures, including enhanced surveillance, contact tracing, and public awareness campaigns to mitigate the spread.\n\nThe recurrent nature of Nipah virus outbreaks in Kerala is attributed to the large population of bats in the region, which frequently harbor the virus. The state's well-developed public health system has been instrumental in detecting and managing these outbreaks effectively. Public health responses have included multisectoral coordination, hospital preparedness, and infection prevention measures to protect healthcare workers and the community.\n\nOverall, the history of Nipah virus outbreaks in Kerala underscores the importance of early detection, public awareness, and robust health infrastructure to combat this zoonotic disease.

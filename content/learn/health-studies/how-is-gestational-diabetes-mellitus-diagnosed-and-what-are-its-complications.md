question: >-
    How is gestational diabetes mellitus diagnosed and what are its complications?
answer: >-
    Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM) is defined as any degree of glucose intolerance that is first recognized during pregnancy. It is the most common complication during pregnancy, and its diagnosis typically occurs between 24 and 28 weeks of gestation. \n\n### Diagnosis of Gestational Diabetes Mellitus\n\nThe diagnosis of GDM can be made using different screening methods, with the most common being the two-step approach recommended by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG). This involves:\n\n1. **Initial Screening**: A 50 g, 1-hour glucose challenge test is performed. If the blood glucose level exceeds a certain threshold (usually 130 mg/dL or 140 mg/dL), a follow-up test is required.\n2. **Confirmatory Testing**: A 100 g, 3-hour oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) is conducted. The diagnosis of GDM is established if two or more of the following values are met or exceeded:\n   - Fasting: ≥ 95 mg/dL\n   - 1 hour: ≥ 180 mg/dL\n   - 2 hours: ≥ 155 mg/dL\n   - 3 hours: ≥ 140 mg/dL\n\nAdditionally, for women at high risk of developing GDM, screening may occur earlier in the pregnancy.\n\n### Complications of Gestational Diabetes Mellitus\n\nGestational diabetes can lead to several complications for both the mother and the fetus:\n\n#### Maternal Complications:\n- **Increased Risk of Hypertension**: Women with GDM are at a higher risk of developing high blood pressure and preeclampsia, a serious condition characterized by high blood pressure and potential organ damage.\n- **C-Section Delivery**: GDM can lead to larger babies (macrosomia), which may necessitate a cesarean section for delivery.\n- **Future Diabetes Risk**: Women who have had GDM are at an increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life. Follow-up testing is recommended 4 to 12 weeks postpartum and every 1 to 3 years thereafter.\n\n#### Fetal Complications:\n- **Macrosomia**: Babies may grow larger than normal, which can complicate delivery and increase the risk of injury during birth.\n- **Preterm Birth**: There is an increased risk of preterm labor and delivery.\n- **Obesity and Metabolic Disorders**: Children born to mothers with GDM may have a higher risk of obesity and metabolic disorders later in life.\n\nIn summary, gestational diabetes is a significant health concern during pregnancy, necessitating careful screening and management to mitigate risks for both the mother and the child.

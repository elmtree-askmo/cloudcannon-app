question: >-
    What chemical barriers exist in the external defenses of innate immunity?
answer: >-
    In the context of innate immunity, chemical barriers play a crucial role as part of the body's first line of defense against pathogens. These barriers include various substances that help to inhibit the growth and entry of infectious agents. Here are some key chemical barriers involved in innate immunity:\n\n1. **Enzymes**: \n   - **Lysozyme**: Found in saliva, tears, and nasal secretions, lysozyme is an enzyme that can break down the cell walls of certain bacteria, effectively killing them and preventing infection.\n   \n2. **Acids**: \n   - **Hydrochloric Acid**: Present in the stomach, hydrochloric acid creates a highly acidic environment that is hostile to many pathogens, aiding in their destruction during digestion.\n   - **Fatty Acids**: Secreted by sebaceous glands in the skin, fatty acids can inhibit the growth of certain bacteria and fungi.\n\n3. **Antimicrobial Proteins**: \n   - **Defensins and Cathelicidins**: These are small peptides that can disrupt the membranes of bacteria, fungi, and some viruses, leading to their destruction.\n   - **Lactoferrin**: Found in various secretions, lactoferrin binds iron, which is essential for bacterial growth, thereby limiting the availability of this nutrient to pathogens.\n\n4. **Bile Acids**: \n   - Present in the gastrointestinal tract, bile acids can disrupt microbial membranes and have antimicrobial properties.\n\n5. **Transferrin and Lactoferrin**: \n   - These proteins bind iron and can sequester it away from pathogens, limiting their growth.\n\n6. **Mucus**: \n   - Mucus traps pathogens and particles, preventing them from adhering to epithelial surfaces and facilitating their removal from the body.\n\nThese chemical barriers, along with physical barriers such as skin and mucous membranes, form an essential part of the innate immune system, providing immediate and non-specific protection against a wide range of pathogens.

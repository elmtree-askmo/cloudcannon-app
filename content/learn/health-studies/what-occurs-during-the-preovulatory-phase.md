question: >-
    What occurs during the pre-ovulatory phase?
answer: >-
    During the pre-ovulatory phase, also known as the follicular phase or proliferative phase, several key processes occur in preparation for ovulation. This phase typically begins on the first day of menstruation and lasts for about 10 to 17 days, depending on the individual cycle length.\n\n1. **Follicular Development**: The primary focus of the pre-ovulatory phase is the maturation of ovarian follicles. Under the influence of hormones such as follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH), several follicles begin to develop, but usually, only one becomes the dominant follicle that will release an oocyte during ovulation.\n\n2. **Hormonal Changes**: The pre-ovulatory phase is characterized by increasing levels of estrogen produced by the developing follicles. This rise in estrogen is crucial as it stimulates the thickening of the uterine lining in preparation for a potential pregnancy. Although progesterone levels are relatively low during this phase, they begin to rise towards the end as the body prepares for ovulation.\n\n3. **Cervical Mucus Changes**: During this phase, cervical mucus is typically thick and viscous. As ovulation approaches, the mucus becomes more abundant, thinner, and clearer, resembling raw egg white, which facilitates sperm movement through the cervix.\n\n4. **Timing of Ovulation**: The pre-ovulatory phase culminates in ovulation, which occurs around the midpoint of the menstrual cycle (approximately day 14 in a typical 28-day cycle). The peak in estrogen levels triggers a surge in LH, leading to the final maturation of the oocyte and its release from the dominant follicle.\n\nOverall, the pre-ovulatory phase is essential for preparing the body for potential fertilization and pregnancy, setting the stage for the subsequent ovulatory phase.
